1. The \(\alpha \)-scale spaces

Pauwels et al. [4] and later Duits et al. [1] introduced the \(\alpha \)-scale spaces for image processing. The basis of the approach is a generalized heat equation. The resulting convolution kernels can be described best by the tools of fractional calculus. The kernel evolution is governed by two parameters - the scale s and the order of differentiation \(\alpha \). The approach leads to the fractional heat problem: \begin {flalign*} u\left (0, \mathbf {x} \right ) &= I\left ( \mathbf {x} \right ) \\ \partial _s u \left ( s, \mathbf {x} \right ) & = - (-\Delta )^{\alpha } u\left ( s, \mathbf {x} \right ) , \quad 0 \leq \alpha \leq 1 \end {flalign*}

where the Riesz fractional Laplacian operator is defined in the Fourier domain by: \[ (-\Delta )^{\alpha } U ( \mathbf {k} ) := k^{2\alpha } U ( \mathbf {k} ), \quad k= |\mathbf {k}|, \] where the \(k\) is the modulus of the wave vector \(\mathbf {k} \). Formally, the operator is extended by continuity for \(\alpha =1\) as \( (-\Delta )^{1} \mapsto k^2 \), which corresponds to the usual Laplacian; and to identity for \(\alpha =0\), corresponding to invariance in the spatial domain.

2. Differential Invariants

There are several types of geometric features useful for image segmentation. Typical interesting image features are blobs, filaments and corners. The normal Laplacian \(\Delta _{ \perp G}\) presented above is sensitive to blobs, while its complement – the tangential Laplacian \(\Delta _{ || G}\) is sensitive to filaments. The weak Hessian tensor with respect to the kernel G is defined as the tensor product \[ \mathbb {H}_G (F) := \nabla _G \otimes \nabla _G F \] For differentiable signals, the Hessian is symmetric and can be regarded as a metric tensor.

In Cartesian coordinates, this can be specialized to the usual formula \[ \mathbb {H}_G (F)= \left ( \begin {array}{ll} G_{xx} & G_{xy} \\ G_{xy} & G_{yy} \end {array} \right ) \star F \] where subscripts denote differentiation by coordinate variables. Furthermore, for the trace holds \[ \mathrm {Tr} \,\mathbb {H}_G (F) = \nabla _G^2 F = \Delta _{G} F \] where \( \Delta _{G}\) denotes the weak Laplacian operator. The eigenvalues \(\lambda _{1,2}\) can be determined locally from the equation \begin {equation} \mathrm {det} (\mathbb {H}_G (F)- \lambda \mathbb {I} )=0 \end {equation}

Notable differential invariants are the amplitude \(|\nabla _{G} F|\) and the orientation of the gradient, the Hessian determinant, the Hessian eigenvalues, as well as the isophote \(\kappa \) and tangential curvatures \(\mu \) [2]. Up to a sign convention we have \begin {flalign} \kappa & = \nabla _G \cdot \mathbf {n}^0 \\ \mu & = \nabla _G \cdot \mathbf {\tau }, \quad \mathbf {\tau }= \mathbf {n}^{0} \cdot I_2 \end {flalign}

where \(I_2\) is the pseudoscalar of the Euclidean image plane. From the perspective of scale space theory the study of the differential invariants of the image reduce to the study of the differential invariants of the radially symmetric Riesz heat kernel.

3. Orthogonal Laplacian Decomposition (LOD)

The simple representation of the gradient introduced above can be extended to a coordinate independent (!) operator using the tools of Geometric Algebra and Calculus. Readers are directed to [3] for an introductory material on the subject. The Laplace operator can be decomposed in two orthogonal components– on in the direction of the isophote and the other in the direction normal to the isophotes. The Laplacian of scalar function F can be decomposed into a normal component and tangential component, thus breaking the isotropy of the original operator \[ \nabla ^2 F = \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla \right )^2 F + \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla F\right ) \nabla \cdot \mathbf {n}^0, \] where \(\mathbf {n} =\nabla F\) is the gradient, \(\mathbf {n}^0\) is the unit normal vector to the isophote curve \( F (x,y) =c\), \[ \quad \mathbf {n}^0 = {\mathbf {n} }/{|\mathbf {n} |} \] and \( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla \) denotes the directional derivative. Then \begin {flalign} \Delta _{\perp G} &= \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla _G\right )^2 \\ \Delta _{|| G} &= \left ( \nabla _G \cdot \mathbf {n}^0\right ) \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla _G \right ) \end {flalign}

This is a coordinate-free definition of \(\Delta _{\perp }\) and \(\Delta _{|| }\), which can be specialized to any smooth coordinate system. This comes in contrast to the approach of ”gauge coordinates” employed in [2]. For example, in Cartesian coordinates the LOD can be represented by \begin {flalign} \left ( G_x^2+ G_y^2 \right ) \Delta _{ \perp G} & = \left ( G_x^2 \right ) G_{xx} + \left ( 2 G_x G_y \right ) G_{xy} + \left ( G_y ^2\right ) G_{yy} \label {ed:lapo} \\ \left ( G_x^2+ G_y^2 \right ) \Delta _{|| G} &= \left ( G_x^2\right ) G_{xx} - \left ( 2 G_x G_y \right ) G_{xy} + \left ( G_y ^2\right ) G_{yy} \label {ed:lapt} \end {flalign}
